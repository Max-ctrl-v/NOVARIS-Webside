Why BSFZ Applications Are Rejected

If you have received a rejection notice from the Federal Certification Body (BSFZ), you are not alone. Approximately 25-30% of all initial applications are partially or fully rejected. This means: a significant proportion of companies that are actually entitled to the R&D Tax Credit initially fail in the BSFZ process.

The reasons are varied, but in most cases the problem is not that no research and development (R&D) is taking place. Rather, the R&D character of the projects is not made sufficiently clear in the application. The BSFZ evaluates applications based on strict criteria derived from the OECD Frascati Manual. Those who do not precisely address these criteria risk rejection – regardless of how innovative the project actually is.

Important to know: A BSFZ rejection is not a final verdict. You have the right to file an objection, and the chances of success are very good with well-founded argumentation. With professional support, approximately 70-80% of objections are successfully resolved.

The most common reason for rejections is the unclear distinction between R&D activities and routine activities. Many companies describe their projects from a business perspective – they explain what they are building or developing and why it makes business sense. However, the BSFZ is exclusively interested in the technical-scientific dimension: What technical uncertainties existed? What new knowledge was generated? What systematic approach was applied?

This discrepancy between what companies intuitively describe and what the BSFZ must evaluate is the main cause of avoidable rejections. With the right presentation approach, this error can be corrected in an objection or improved new application.

The 6 Most Common Reasons for a BSFZ Rejection

To formulate a successful objection, you must first understand why your application was rejected. Here are the six most common rejection reasons we see in our consulting practice:

1. No Recognizable Technical Novelty

This is the most common rejection reason. The BSFZ classifies the project as an application of already known methods, processes, or technologies. From the evaluators' perspective, there is no evidence that something technically new is being created that goes beyond the general state of the art.

How to address this in your objection: Describe precisely how your solution differs from the state of the art. Conduct an explicit state-of-the-art review and show the gap that your project fills. Reference technical publications, patent searches, or industry-specific standards that demonstrate that no ready-made solution exists.

2. Lack of Systematic Approach

The BSFZ requires evidence that R&D work is conducted systematically and in a planned manner – following scientific methodology with hypothesis formation, experiments, analysis, and documentation. Many applications describe the work as an iterative development process without highlighting the systematic character.

How to address this in your objection: Present your development process as a structured research plan. Describe concrete work packages, milestones, test series, and documentation methods. Show that results are systematically recorded and analyzed.

3. No Technical Uncertainty

R&D under the FZulG requires that the outcome was not foreseeable in advance. If the BSFZ gains the impression that the project outcome was predictable or routinely achievable, there is no R&D project. This reason occurs particularly frequently with software projects and process optimizations.

How to address this in your objection: Describe specifically what technical challenges existed. Which approaches did not work? Which alternative solution paths were tested? Document failed attempts, unexpected results, and the need for adjustments. Technical uncertainty is demonstrated precisely in the problems that arose during development.

4. Insufficient Project Description

Too vague, too short, no concrete details – many rejections simply result from an inadequate application description. BSFZ evaluators can only assess what is stated in the application. If important details are missing, rejection is the default.

How to address this in your objection: Significantly expand the project description. Specify concrete technical parameters, materials, algorithms, processes, and measurement results. A good BSFZ description typically has 3-5 pages per project and contains technical details that even a non-specialist evaluator can follow.

5. Wrong R&D Category Selected

The FZulG distinguishes between basic research, industrial research, and experimental development. If you select the wrong category – beispielsweise Grundlagenforschung angeben, obwohl es sich um experimentelle Entwicklung handelt – this can lead to follow-up questions or rejections. The categorization influences which evaluation criteria the assessors apply.

How to address this in your objection: Review the classification based on official definitions. Most company projects fall under "experimental development." Ensure that your project description matches the chosen category and meets the respective criteria.

6. Routine Activity Instead of R&D

Quality assurance, standard tests, market research, routine software modifications, or pure product maintenance are often declared as R&D but are not according to BSFZ criteria. This error occurs particularly in companies that define R&D broadly and also include regular engineering activities.

How to address this in your objection: Clearly distinguish R&D activities from routine work. Remove non-eligible activities from the application and focus on project components that actually demonstrate technical novelty and uncertainty. Less is often more here: a focused application is stronger than a broad but superficial one.

Practical tip: Analyze the rejection notice word by word. The BSFZ usually states specific reasons for the rejection. Your objection must address exactly these points – nothing more and nothing less. A general revision is not sufficient; the objection must address the identified deficits with surgical precision.

What Can You Do After a BSFZ Rejection?

After receiving a rejection notice, you have three fundamental options available. Which option is right depends on your individual situation – particularly the deadline situation, the type of rejection, and the available evidence.

Option 1: Formal Objection (Within One Month)

The formal objection is the most direct and effective remedy against a BSFZ rejection. The deadline is one month from notification of the rejection. Within this deadline, you can file a written objection and submit an improved justification.

The objection should contain the following elements:

  • A written justification that specifically addresses the rejection reasons stated in the notice
  • A revised and more detailed project description
  • Additional evidence for the technical novelty and R&D character of the project
  • If applicable, a supplementary technical statement

Important: An objection can also relate to partial rejections. For example, if only three of five submitted projects were rejected, you can specifically challenge the rejection of those three projects.

Option 2: Improved New Application

If the objection deadline has already passed or if you want to fundamentally revise the application, you can submit an improved new application. This is also advisable if you want to apply for other or new fiscal years.

A new application offers the advantage that you can set up the entire application from scratch – with the experience from the first rejection. With professional support, the chances of success with an improved new application are significantly higher than with the original first application, as the typical pitfalls are already known.

Option 3: Lawsuit Before the Tax Court

As a last resort, you have the option of legal action before the tax court. However, this option is rarely necessary and is only advisable for fundamental disputes, where a fundamental clarification is sought. In practice, most cases are already successfully resolved in the objection process.

Before pursuing legal action, you should seek legal advice and carefully weigh the cost-benefit ratio. The objection is in most cases the more efficient and cost-effective path.

Guide: Successful Appeal in 5 Steps

A successful objection against a BSFZ rejection follows a structured process. Here we show you the five decisive steps that maximize your chances of success:

1

Analyze the Rejection Notice

Read the rejection notice carefully and identify the exact reasons for the rejection. The BSFZ usually formulates its reasoning very specifically – every stated point must be addressed in the objection. Highlight the key statements and assign them to the rejection categories mentioned above. Pay particular attention to phrases such as "not recognizable," "not demonstrated," or "insufficiently presented" – these show you where the argumentation needs to be strengthened.

2

Revise the Project Description

Revise your project description specifically based on the identified weaknesses. Every rejection reason must be refuted with concrete, technically sound argumentation. Add details that emphasize the R&D character: Which technical alternatives were examined? Which results were unexpected? Where was the technical uncertainty? Use the terminology of the Frascati Manual and ensure that the five criteria for R&D are clearly addressed: novelty, uncertainty, systematic approach, transferability, and reproducibility.

3

Gather Additional Evidence

Support the R&D character of your project with concrete evidence. The more you can document, the stronger your argumentation becomes. Possible evidence includes: patent applications or patent searches, scientific publications that demonstrate the novelty area of your project, records of test series and experiments, prototype documentation, measurement results and test reports, correspondence with research institutions or reviewers, and comparisons with the state of the art based on specialist literature.

4

Draft a Technical Statement

Draft a clear, structured technical statement that addresses the reasons for rejection point by point. Argue objectively and with technical precision. Avoid marketing-style superlatives and focus on facts. The statement should be understandable for a non-specialist reviewer, yet demonstrate the technical depth that proves the R&D character. Ideally, follow this structure: (a) Quote the reason for rejection, (b) Technical counter-statement with evidence, (c) Conclusion explaining why R&D exists.

5

Submit Within the Deadline

Submit the objection within the one-month deadline. Plan sufficient lead time – a thorough revision typically takes 1-2 weeks. If time is running short, first submit an informal objection to preserve the deadline and provide the detailed justification later. Ensure that all documents are complete and the argumentation is coherent and comprehensible. An objection with gaps or contradictory statements does more harm than good.

Common mistake: Many companies simply resubmit the original application with minimal adjustments. This is not sufficient. The BSFZ expects a substantial revision that demonstrates you have understood the reasons for rejection and can refute them with concrete arguments. The more thorough your objection, the higher the probability of success.
