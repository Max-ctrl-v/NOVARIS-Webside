What Is the R&D Tax Credit (Forschungszulage)?

The Forschungszulage (R&D tax credit) is a tax-based incentive for research and development (R&D) activities in Germany. It was introduced under the Forschungszulagengesetz (FZulG) and has been available to all taxable companies since January 1, 2020 – regardless of industry, size, or profitability. This makes the Forschungszulage the first cross-industry, tax-based research funding instrument in German history.

The legal basis is Section 2 FZulG (Forschungszulagengesetz), which defines the eligible research and development projects. Activities in the following three categories qualify:

  • Basic research: Experimental or theoretical work primarily aimed at acquiring new knowledge, without any direct practical application in mind.
  • Industrial research (applied research): Planned research to acquire new knowledge and skills with the goal of developing new products, processes, or services, or substantially improving existing ones.
  • Experimental development: The acquisition, combination, design, and use of existing scientific, technical, economic, and other relevant knowledge and skills with the goal of developing new or improved products, processes, or services.

What makes the R&D tax credit under the FZulG unique: Unlike traditional funding programs (such as ZIM or KMU-innovativ), the Forschungszulage is not subject to a competitive process. Every company that meets the requirements has a legal entitlement to the funding. There are no limited funding pools and no application deadlines. The R&D tax credit is offset against income tax or corporate tax – and in case of a tax surplus, it is even paid out directly.

Good to know: The Forschungszulage is not a grant but a tax allowance. It is credited against the assessed income or corporate tax. If the allowance exceeds the tax liability, the difference is paid out to the company. This makes it particularly attractive for companies in loss phases or startups.

Who Can Apply for the R&D Tax Credit?

The tax-based research funding under the Forschungszulagengesetz is generally available to all taxable companies in Germany. Eligible entities include:

  • Corporations (GmbH, AG, UG) with unlimited corporate tax liability
  • Partnerships (GbR, OHG, KG, GmbH & Co. KG) through their partners
  • Sole proprietors and freelancers with income from trade or self-employment
  • Permanent establishments of foreign companies in Germany
  • Co-entrepreneurships as defined in Section 15(1)(1)(2) EStG

The key principle: The R&D tax credit is industry- and size-independent. Both small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and large enterprises and corporations can apply. Since the Wachstumschancengesetz (Growth Opportunities Act), SMEs even benefit from an increased funding rate of 35% instead of 25%.

Special Rules for Affiliated Companies

For affiliated companies as defined in Section 15 AktG or the EU SME definition, an important restriction applies: The maximum assessment base of EUR 12 million is shared across all affiliated companies. This means that if several entities belong to a group, they share the cap. This applies to both horizontal and vertical corporate affiliations.

Requirements at a Glance

To apply for the R&D tax credit, the following requirements must be met:

  • Unlimited or limited tax liability in Germany
  • Conducting own R&D projects or commissioning contract R&D
  • The R&D activity must meet the criteria of Section 2 FZulG (novelty, uncertainty, systematic approach, transferability, reproducibility)
  • No double funding of the same project through other state aid for the same eligible expenditures
Important: The R&D tax credit can also be claimed if the company generates no profit. In this case, the allowance is paid out as a tax refund. This represents a significant liquidity advantage, especially for startups and growing companies.

How Much Is the R&D Tax Credit in 2026?

The R&D tax credit amount has increased several times since the FZulG was introduced in 2020. Since the Wachstumschancengesetz (Growth Opportunities Act) 2024 and the adjustments for 2025/2026, the following key figures apply:

Maximum assessment base: EUR 12,000,000 per fiscal year

Standard funding rate: 25% of eligible expenditures
SME funding rate: 35% of eligible expenditures (since 2024)

Maximum R&D tax credit: up to EUR 4,200,000 per year (SME at full utilization)
Standard: up to EUR 3,000,000 per year

The Calculation in Detail

The R&D tax credit is calculated using the following formula: Eligible expenditures x Funding rate = R&D tax credit. The eligible expenditures comprise various cost categories (see next section). The key calculation parameters for 2026:

  • Personnel costs: Gross wages and salaries of employees working on R&D projects, proportional to the time spent on the respective project
  • Owner contributions: A flat rate of EUR 70 per hour for sole proprietors and partners who personally conduct research (maximum 40 hours per week)
  • Overhead allowance: From 2026, 20% of personnel costs can be applied as a flat-rate overhead – a significant improvement over previous years
  • Contract research: 60% of the fee paid to the contractor

Calculation Example: SME with 5 R&D Employees

R&D personnel costs: EUR 400,000/year
+ Overhead allowance (20%): EUR 80,000
= Assessment base: EUR 480,000
x SME funding rate (35%): = EUR 168,000 R&D tax credit

For a standard enterprise (25%): EUR 120,000 R&D tax credit.

Which Expenditures Are Eligible?

The Forschungszulagengesetz defines in Section 3 FZulG exactly which expenditures are included in the assessment base for the R&D tax credit. Not every expense related to research and development is automatically eligible. Here is a detailed overview:

1. Personnel Costs (Own Staff)

By far the most important cost category is the wages and salaries of employees engaged in R&D. Eligible are the taxable gross wages and salaries received by the employee, including:

  • Base salary and allowances
  • Overtime compensation
  • Holiday and Christmas bonuses (proportional)
  • Performance bonuses and premiums (if taxable)

Not eligible are employer social security contributions, company pension schemes, benefits in kind, or non-cash benefits. Personnel costs are considered proportionally based on the actual time spent on the R&D project. Careful time tracking is therefore essential.

2. Contract Research

If a company commissions a third party for R&D activities, 60% of the fee paid qualifies as eligible expenditure. This applies both to commissioning research institutions (universities, Fraunhofer institutes) and to commissioning other companies. The contractor may not claim their own R&D tax credit for the same expenditures.

Practical tip: The 60% rule for contract research does not mean that 40% is lost. The legislator assumes that 40% of the fee covers non-eligible material costs and the contractor's profit margin. Clearly document the R&D portion of contract research in the agreement.

3. Owner Contributions by Sole Proprietors and Partners

Sole proprietors and partners of partnerships who personally conduct research can claim their contributions at a flat rate of EUR 70 per hour. The maximum weekly working time is capped at 40 hours. This yields a maximum annual assessment base for owner contributions of approximately EUR 145,600 (at 52 weeks x 40 hours x EUR 70).

4. Overhead Allowance (New from 2026)

One of the most important innovations: From fiscal year 2026, companies can apply an overhead allowance of 20% on eligible personnel costs and owner contributions. This covers costs for lab materials, consumables, software licenses, and other overheads on a flat-rate basis that could not previously be claimed.

Non-Eligible Expenditures

Explicitly not eligible under the FZulG are:

  • Material costs (materials, machines, equipment) – except through the overhead allowance
  • Depreciation on research facilities
  • Travel costs
  • Patent costs and approval fees
  • Market launch costs
  • Routine quality controls and tests

The Application Process in 2 Steps

The procedure for claiming the R&D tax credit is a two-stage process. First, the substantive R&D qualification of the projects is reviewed by an independent body, then the tax application is submitted to the tax office. Both steps are mandatory.

1

BSFZ Certificate: Application to the Certification Body for the R&D Tax Credit

In the first step, you must submit a BSFZ application to the Bescheinigungsstelle Forschungszulage (BSFZ) – the certification body for the R&D tax credit. The BSFZ is an entity affiliated with the BMBF (Federal Ministry of Education and Research), operated by a consortium of VDI Technology Center, DLR Project Management Agency, and AiF Projekt GmbH.

The application is submitted exclusively electronically via the BSFZ portal (ELSTER-based). The following information and documents are required:

  • Description of the R&D project (objectives, methods, planned outcomes)
  • Presentation of the scientific or technical uncertainty
  • Evidence of novelty compared to the state of the art
  • Project period and participating employees
  • Classification into basic research, industrial research, or experimental development

The BSFZ exclusively assesses whether the project meets the substantive criteria under Section 2 FZulG. Processing typically takes 4 to 12 weeks. Upon positive assessment, you receive a certificate to present to the tax office.

2

Application to the Tax Office: Assessment of the R&D Tax Credit

With the BSFZ certificate in hand, you submit the application for assessment of the R&D tax credit to your local tax office. This is done through the Annex FZ (Anlage FZ) to the income or corporate tax return, or as a separate application under Section 6 FZulG.

In the tax office application, you declare the eligible expenditures – i.e., the actual personnel costs, contract research costs, and owner contributions incurred. The tax office reviews the mathematical accuracy and assesses the R&D tax credit. The credit is applied against the next income or corporate tax assessment.

Important notes on the tax office application:

  • The application must be submitted after the end of the fiscal year in which the expenditures were incurred
  • The BSFZ certificate must be attached to the application
  • Detailed breakdown of eligible expenditures per project
  • Evidence of time tracking for proportionally considered personnel costs
  • For contract research: submission of contracts and invoices
Timeline: The BSFZ certificate can be applied for during the current fiscal year – you do not need to wait until the project is completed. We recommend applying for the BSFZ certificate as early as possible to gain planning certainty.

Claiming the R&D Tax Credit Retroactively

Many companies are unaware that they can also claim the R&D tax credit retroactively for past fiscal years. In fact, retroactive claims are possible for all open tax years from 2020 onwards – the year the FZulG came into effect.

Requirements for Retroactive Claims

For a retroactive R&D tax credit claim to be filed, the following conditions must be met:

  • The R&D project was conducted in the respective fiscal year (from 2020)
  • The eligible expenditures are verifiable (personnel costs, time records, contracts)
  • The tax assessment for the relevant year is not yet legally binding or can be corrected via an amendment request
  • No BSFZ certificate was applied for? No problem – the certificate can also be applied for retroactively

Which Assessment Limits Apply Retroactively?

For retroactive claims, the rules in effect at the respective time apply:

2020–2023: Max. assessment base EUR 4M, funding rate 25%, max. credit EUR 1M

2024 (after Wachstumschancengesetz): Max. assessment base EUR 12M, funding rate 25% (SME: 35%), max. credit EUR 3–4.2M

From 2026: Additionally 20% overhead allowance on personnel costs

For companies that have been continuously conducting R&D since 2020 and have not yet claimed the R&D tax credit, substantial back payments may result. A mid-sized company with annual R&D personnel costs of EUR 500,000 could, for example, receive more than EUR 500,000 in retroactive R&D tax credits.

2026 Updates: What Has Changed?

The Forschungszulagengesetz has been amended several times since its introduction. The most important changes in effect in 2026 are primarily based on the Wachstumschancengesetz (Growth Opportunities Act) (enacted 2024) and further adjustments. Here is an overview of all key updates:

Increased Assessment Base: From EUR 4M to EUR 12M

The most significant change: The maximum assessment base has been raised from the original EUR 2 million (2020) through EUR 4 million (2021–2023) to the current EUR 12 million per fiscal year. This represents a sixfold increase in the maximum funding amount. Research-intensive mid-sized companies and large enterprises benefit substantially.

SME Bonus: 35% Instead of 25% Funding Rate

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) under the EU definition have received an increased funding rate of 35% instead of the standard 25% since 2024. The SME definition covers companies with fewer than 250 employees and either annual revenue of no more than EUR 50 million or a balance sheet total of no more than EUR 43 million. This bonus increases the maximum credit for SMEs to up to EUR 4.2 million per year.

20% Overhead Allowance from 2026

For the first time, from fiscal year 2026, companies can apply a flat-rate overhead component of 20% on eligible personnel costs and owner contributions. Previously, only direct personnel costs and contract research were eligible. The new allowance covers indirect costs such as lab rent, consumables, software licenses, and similar operating costs directly related to R&D activities.

Owner Contribution Rate Adjusted

The hourly flat rate for owner contributions by sole proprietors and partners has been set at EUR 70 per hour. The maximum weekly working time of 40 hours remains unchanged. For many owner-managed companies, this represents a substantial funding opportunity that has not yet been fully utilized.

Summary of Changes

What do the updates mean in practice?

An SME with EUR 1 million in R&D personnel costs can calculate as follows from 2026:
Personnel costs: EUR 1,000,000
+ 20% overhead allowance: EUR 200,000
= Assessment base: EUR 1,200,000
x 35% (SME rate): = EUR 420,000 R&D tax credit

For comparison: In 2020, the same company would have received only EUR 250,000 (EUR 1M x 25%, no overhead allowance).
