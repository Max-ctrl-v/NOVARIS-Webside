Cleantech Projects and
the FZulG

New operating principle or concept

The technology goes beyond known approaches – not merely assembling standard components.

Technical uncertainty

The efficiency, service life or system stability was unknown at the outset.

Systematic documentation

Experiments, measurement series, simulations and iterations were conducted and recorded in a structured manner.

Independent financial risk

The company bears the cost risk of the R&D – not the client or funding body alone.

Important for energy companies

Industry-Specific

Industry-Specific Requirements for Energy & Cleantech

The energy transition is driving one of the most extensive technological transformations of our time – creating enormous potential for the research allowance (Forschungszulage). Grid integration and smart grid R&D rank among the most classic R&D topics in the sector. The increasing feed-in of volatile renewable energy sources demands novel control algorithms that ensure grid stability in real time – while the share of rotating masses in the system continues to decline. Development work on predictive grid management systems that combine weather data, consumption forecasts, and storage availability into feed-in schedules typically meets all BSFZ criteria for experimental development. Research on decentralised energy management systems (DEMS), virtual power plants, and blockchain-based peer-to-peer energy trading platforms also qualifies under the FZulG, provided technical uncertainties are documented – for example, scaling to millions of prosumer endpoints, latency requirements below 100 ms for primary frequency response, or integrating heterogeneous communication protocols (IEC 61850, OCPP, Modbus TCP) into a unified control system. NOVARIS supports energy suppliers and grid operators in structuring these complex R&D projects so that the experimental character is clearly demonstrated for the BSFZ application.

Energy storage technologies represent one of the most promising funding areas in the energy sector. The development of advanced battery storage – from lithium iron phosphate (LFP) and sodium-ion cells to next-generation solid-state batteries – involves substantial experimental components: materials research on cathode materials, electrolyte optimisation, cell design for improved energy density and cycle life, and the development of intelligent battery management systems (BMS) with AI-driven state estimation (SOC/SOH) and degradation prediction. In the hydrogen domain, electrolyser development (PEM, AEL, SOEC), fuel cell system integration, pressurised storage concepts, and hydrogen logistics solutions are typical R&D activities. Thermal storage systems – latent heat storage, thermochemical storage, high-temperature solid-media storage – likewise require systematic research on storage materials, heat transfer concepts, and system integration. A key point for eligibility: scaling from laboratory prototypes to industrial dimensions (e.g. from a 10 kWh lab cell to a 1 MWh storage system) qualifies as experimental development whenever technical uncertainties must be overcome – for instance regarding thermal management, safety concepts, or degradation behaviour under real operating conditions.

The development of innovative SiC/GaN-based power electronics generates particularly high eligible R&D costs in the energy sector. Wide-bandgap semiconductors such as silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) enable inverters, rectifiers, and DC/DC converters with significantly higher efficiencies, lower switching losses, and more compact form factors – but they require fundamentally new circuit designs, gate driver layouts, thermal architectures, and EMC strategies. Validation of this power electronics under real grid conditions – grid fault ride-through per VDE-AR-N 4120, anti-islanding detection, harmonic compensation per EN 61000-3-12, and reactive power provision – constitutes experimental development under the FZulG. Development of multi-level converter topologies for medium-voltage applications, bidirectional charging stations (V2G), and solid-state transformers also falls within the scope of eligible research fields. Our consultants with electrical engineering backgrounds identify on average 40–55% more eligible R&D components in typical power electronics portfolios than non-specialist generalists.

IEC certification testing is frequently underestimated in the context of the research allowance. Yet IEC certification processes – IEC 61215 and IEC 61730 for PV modules, IEC 61400 for wind turbines, IEC 62109 for inverters, IEC 62619 for stationary battery storage – generate systematic test protocols, experimental results, and measurement data that serve as robust R&D evidence. When technical issues arise during certification that require iterative design adjustments (e.g. failure in damp-heat-freeze testing, insufficient PID resistance, EMC limit exceedances), this constitutes experimental development. NOVARIS helps document these iteration loops cleanly and prepare the knowledge gains for the BSFZ application. Furthermore, renewable energy system optimisation – from aerodynamic rotor blade development for wind turbines to research on perovskite tandem solar cells and efficiency improvements in biomass gasification plants – covers a broad field of eligible activities. The development of monitoring systems for predictive maintenance that use machine learning to forecast failures in wind turbines or PV inverters also typically qualifies as R&D. Our energy technology consultants support the identification of eligible projects along the entire energy value chain – from generation through storage and conversion to intelligent distribution and consumption optimisation.

Typical Funding Amounts in the Energy Sector

Example 1: Mid-Size Energy Tech Manufacturer (80 Employees)

  • • Company size: 80 employees, of which 12 engineers in the R&D department
  • • Gross personnel costs for R&D staff (incl. employer contributions): €840,000 / year
  • • Eligible R&D share (SiC inverter development + smart grid algorithms): approx. 100%
  • • Assessment basis: €840,000
  • • Research allowance (25%): €210,000 / year
  • • Cumulated over 3 years: up to €630,000

Note: Since 2024, the maximum assessment basis is €4 million per fiscal year (or €8 million for SMEs). A company of this size typically does not exhaust the cap, so 100% of eligible personnel costs can be claimed.

Example 2: Cleantech Startup with Owner Contributions (15 Employees)

  • • Company size: 15 employees, shareholder-managing director actively involved in research
  • • 4 employed developers (gross salary: €320,000 / year)
  • • Owner contribution (Eigenleistung): €70 / hour × 30 hrs/week × 46 weeks = €96,600 / year
  • • Eligible assessment basis: €320,000 + €96,600 = €416,600
  • • Research allowance (25%): €104,150 / year
  • • SME bonus (additional 10%): + €41,660 / year
  • • Total research allowance: €145,810 / year

Note: Since 2024, SMEs (under 250 employees, < €50 million revenue) can claim an increased rate of 35% instead of 25% on the assessment basis. The owner contribution of a researching shareholder-managing director is capped at €70 / hour (§ 3(3) FZulG).
