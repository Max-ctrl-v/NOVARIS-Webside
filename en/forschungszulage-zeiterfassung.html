Time Tracking for the Research Allowance (Forschungszulage) – How to Document Properly

Correct tracking of R&D working hours is crucial for the amount of your Research Allowance (Forschungszulage). Learn what requirements apply and how to avoid typical mistakes.

Why Is Time Tracking for the Research Allowance (Forschungszulage) So Important?

Time tracking is the backbone of every successful R&D Tax Credit application under the Research Allowance Act (FZulG). The reason is simple: eligible personnel costs – i.e., the gross wages and salaries of employees working in research and development – constitute by far the largest share of the assessment basis. And these personnel costs must be determined and documented proportionally based on the actual time spent on R&D projects.

When assessing the R&D Tax Credit, the tax office checks whether the claimed personnel costs can be plausibly and traceably allocated to the certified R&D projects. Without proper time tracking, there is no basis for this allocation – with the consequence that the tax office reduces the assessment basis or, in the worst case, rejects the application entirely.

Time tracking becomes particularly relevant in the following situations:

  • Faulty documentation leads to reductions: If the tax office cannot verify the claimed hours, the assessment basis is reduced. In practice, this means you are giving away money because the documentation is incorrect – even though the R&D activity actually took place.
  • Retroactive applications require reconstruction: The R&D Tax Credit can be applied for retroactively for all open tax years from 2020. For past years, project-based time tracking often does not exist. In these cases, the time allocation must be reconstructed using alternative sources – a process that is possible but significantly more effort than ongoing tracking.
  • Time tracking determines funding amount: The R&D Tax Credit is 25% (SMEs: 35%) of eligible expenditures. Every hour not correctly documented reduces your assessment basis and thus directly the amount of your R&D Tax Credit. With an average gross salary of EUR 60,000 per year and an SME rate of 35%, one unrecorded full-time position represents a loss of up to EUR 25,200 in R&D Tax Credit – per year.
  • Tax audits: During tax audits, the tax office can examine time tracking records in detail. Those who cannot present solid documentation risk not only a reduction of the R&D Tax Credit but potentially also additional tax demands.
Key takeaway: Time tracking is not bureaucratic red tape – it is the central piece of evidence for your R&D Tax Credit entitlement. Careful, project-based documentation can make the difference between fully utilizing the funding and a significant reduction.

What the Tax Office Expects from Time Tracking

The Research Allowance Act itself does not contain detailed requirements for the form of time tracking. The requirements instead derive from general tax documentation obligations, guidelines from the BMF (Federal Ministry of Finance), and the audit practices of tax offices. The following principles have become established as standard:

Project-Based Tracking

The most important requirement: Every recorded working hour must be assignable to a specific R&D project. The project must match the one submitted to and certified by the BSFZ (Federal Certification Body for Research Allowance). A blanket statement like "40% of working time for R&D" without project assignment is not sufficient.

Daily or Weekly Recording

Time tracking should ideally be daily. In practice, weekly recording is also accepted by most tax offices, provided it is consistent and plausible. Purely monthly or even quarterly recording is considered too imprecise and carries significant risks during an audit.

Separation of R&D Activities and Routine Work

It must be clearly identifiable which activities are classified as R&D and which belong to normal business operations. This distinction is particularly important for employees who only work part-time on R&D projects. Typical examples of this distinction:

  • R&D activity: Development of a new image recognition algorithm, construction of a prototype with novel material combinations, conducting test series to validate a new process
  • Not R&D activity: Routine software maintenance, quality control using standard procedures, customer support, administrative tasks

Plausibility Check

The tax office compares the stated R&D hours with the total working time of each employee. If an employee works 40 hours per week according to their employment contract but claims 45 hours on R&D projects, this is flagged. Likewise, it is checked whether the number of claimed employees is plausible in relation to the company size.

Signature and Confirmation

Time tracking records should be confirmed (signed or digitally approved) by the respective employee and ideally also by the project manager or supervisor. This significantly increases the credibility of the documentation and is an important aspect during tax audits.

Practical tip: Always align your time tracking with the BSFZ certificate. The projects and project durations described there are the benchmark against which the tax office measures your time tracking. Discrepancies between the BSFZ certificate and time tracking are one of the most common reasons for follow-up questions.

R&D Time Tracking Methods Compared

In practice, three fundamental methods of R&D time tracking have been established, differing in accuracy, effort, and acceptance by the tax office. The choice of the right method depends on company size, existing IT infrastructure, and whether current or retroactive periods need to be documented.

Recommended

1. Project-Based Hourly Tracking

With this method, each employee records daily or weekly the hours worked on a specific R&D project. Recording is done via a time tracking tool, project management software, or a structured Excel template with project codes.

  • Accuracy: High – exact allocation of every hour to a project
  • Tax office acceptance: Very good – the best basis for audit-proof documentation
  • Effort: Medium to high – requires employee discipline and regular review
  • Suitable for: Companies with structured R&D departments, multiple parallel projects, medium and large enterprises
Acceptable

2. Estimation Method with Plausibility Check

With the estimation method, an employee's R&D share is set as a flat rate, e.g., "60% of working time for Project X." However, this estimate must be substantiated with additional evidence – such as project plans, milestone reports, meeting minutes, or progress reports.

  • Accuracy: Medium – less detailed than hourly tracking
  • Tax office acceptance: Acceptable with consistent and traceable documentation
  • Effort: Low to medium – less effort for employees, but more effort for substantiation
  • Suitable for: Small companies with few R&D employees, companies with clearly defined R&D roles
For Retroactive Applications

3. Retrospective Reconstruction

For retroactive applications for past fiscal years (2020–2025), project-based time tracking often does not exist. In this case, the time allocation must be reconstructed retrospectively. Sources include:

  • Project plans and milestone reports
  • Email threads and calendar entries
  • Git commits, Jira/Confluence tickets, version control
  • Project meeting minutes
  • Travel expense reports for project appointments
  • Sworn declarations by involved employees

Retrospective reconstruction requires particular care and experience, as the documentation must be complete and internally consistent. Recommendation: For current fiscal years, project-based hourly tracking is the gold standard. For retroactive periods, retrospective reconstruction is often the only option – but with professional support, it is certainly successful. In any case, consistency is key: all documents must present a coherent overall picture.

Recommended Tools for R&D Time Tracking

The choice of the right tool depends on the size of your company, existing IT infrastructure, and the number of your R&D employees. Three categories can be distinguished:

ERP Systems with Project Module

Companies already using an ERP system such as SAP, Microsoft Dynamics, or DATEV can often use the integrated project time tracking. The advantage: data is directly linked to payroll accounting, which significantly simplifies the later calculation of the assessment basis. Time tracking is automatically consistent with salary data – an important plus during tax office review.

Dedicated Time Tracking Tools

Specialized solutions such as Clockodo, TimeTac, Personio, or Toggl offer project-based time tracking with detailed reporting capabilities. Many of these tools feature export functions, audit trails, and approval workflows that are particularly useful for FZulG documentation. When selecting, look for:

  • Project reference: Ability to assign times to specific projects (and ideally BSFZ project numbers)
  • Export function: Structured data export (CSV, PDF) for submission to the tax office
  • Audit trail: Traceability of when entries were created or modified
  • Approval workflow: Ability for confirmation by supervisors/project managers

Simple Solutions for Small Companies

For companies with few R&D employees, a structured Excel template with project codes can also be sufficient. The key is that the template contains all required information: employee name, calendar week/day, project designation (matching the BSFZ certificate), hours, and a signature line. NOVARIS provides a free FZulG-compliant Excel template as part of our consulting services.

What to look for when choosing a tool:

Regardless of the chosen solution, four criteria must be met: (1) Project-based allocation of working hours, (2) immutability or versioning of entries after the fact (audit trail), (3) ability for structured data export for the tax office, and (4) archiving capability for at least 10 years. If your existing tool meets these criteria, you do not need to implement a new system.

The 5 Most Common Mistakes in Time Tracking – and How to Avoid Them

From our many years of consulting practice, we know the typical pitfalls companies encounter with R&D time tracking. We see the following five mistakes repeatedly – and all of them can be avoided with the right measures.

Mistake 1: No Separation Between R&D and Routine Work

The most common mistake: employees record their total working time, but without distinguishing between R&D activities and routine work. However, the tax office requires clear separation. If a software developer works on both a new AI algorithm (R&D) and bug fixes in ongoing operations (not R&D), these activities must be recorded separately.

Solution: Define clear criteria for which activities qualify as R&D and which do not. Train your employees accordingly and use separate project numbers for R&D and non-R&D in your time tracking.

Mistake 2: Too Coarse Tracking

Purely monthly tracking ("In January I worked about 80 hours on Project X") is increasingly seen as insufficient by tax offices. The coarser the tracking, the greater the risk that plausibility is questioned.

Solution: Track at least weekly, ideally daily. With weekly tracking, hours per project and calendar week should be documented.

Mistake 3: Missing Project Reference

Some companies do track R&D hours but assign them to no specific project. Instead, "R&D" or "Development" is used as a blanket category. This does not meet the requirements – time tracking must be broken down to the individual projects certified by the BSFZ.

Solution: Maintain a separate project number for each BSFZ project in your time tracking. The designations should be consistent with the project titles in the BSFZ certificate.

Mistake 4: Not Including Part-Time Employees and Working Students

Many companies only track the R&D hours of their full-time employees and forget that part-time employees, working students, trainees, and temporary staff are also eligible – provided they work on R&D projects. Working students and doctoral candidates in particular often make a significant R&D contribution that goes unclaimed.

Solution: Review your entire workforce for R&D involvement. Also track the working hours of part-time employees, working students, and interns, provided they work on certified projects.

Mistake 5: Time Tracking Introduced Only Retroactively

Especially with retroactive applications, we repeatedly see companies that have not maintained project-based time tracking for years and now want to create it "after the fact." While this is possible (see Method 3: Retrospective Reconstruction), it requires significantly more effort and carries the risk of lower accuracy.

Solution: Begin immediately with structured time tracking for all current and future R&D projects. For past years, have the reconstruction done by an experienced FZulG consultant who knows the requirements of the tax offices.

Checklist: Is Your Time Tracking FZulG-Compliant?

Use the following checklist to verify whether your current time tracking meets the requirements for the R&D Tax Credit. If you can answer "Yes" to all points, you are on the safe side.

Project-based allocation of all R&D hours to individual BSFZ projects
Daily or weekly recording of working hours (not just monthly)
Clear separation between R&D activities and non-R&D activities
Signature/approval by the respective employee and project manager
Archiving for at least 10 years in accordance with tax retention requirements
Consistency with BSFZ project descriptions – project names and durations match
Inclusion of all relevant employees incl. part-time staff, working students, and apprentices
